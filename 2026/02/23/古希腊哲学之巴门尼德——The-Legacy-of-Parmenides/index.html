Introduction to the Paperback Version

Unlike some commentators,Curd does not take Parmenides to have forbidden or denied the possibility of genuine cosmology.Rather,his aim is to criticize previous accounts of the nature of things while formulating metatheoretical requirements for an acceptable cosmological account.

Such an account must be grounded in metaphysically genuine entities and use mechanisms that do not undermine the reality of those basic things.Parmenides argues that what-is is in such a way that it does not come to be,pass away,or alter,and that anything that is must be a whole of a single kind.Only what genuinely is in this way can be an object of genuine thought and so of knowledge or understanding.Anything that is in the way sanctioned by the arguments of B8 counts as metaphysically basic and could serve as the foundation for a theoretically acceptable account of the world that we perceive.

Monisms

Curd argues that Parmenides advocates predicational monism(a monism about natures or kinds)rather than numerical monism.Numerical,or strict,monism is the claim that there exists only one thing,and this is the monism that has traditionally been attributed to Parmenides.Material monism,the view that everything is or comes from a single material stuff,was attributed to the earliest Greek thinkers by Aristotle.But Curd claims :

each thing that is can be only one thing;it can hold only the one predicate which indicates what it is,and must be hold it in a particularly strong way.To be a genuine entity,a thing must be a predicational unity,with a single account of what it is;but it need not be the case that there exists only one such thing.

So anything that is in the way that the proofs of B8 demand will be something that genuinely is.The claim that X is Y,or that Y is what it is to be X,asserts that X is a basic reality,and that its nature is to be Y.Anything that is must be one,but there may be more than one such thing.

Internal and External Negations

One might agree that what-is must be a whole of a single kind and still argue that this entails that there can exist only one such thing:if there were two or more things,each could be said to be different from the others,and so we would be led to the conclusion that what-is is not.

There are two ways to interpret this objection:First,one should view the collection so named as something apart from the things named.Just there is no thing,being,apart from the things that are,and being is therefore not divisible in the way envisioned by this version of the objection.
